# The Distraction Machine Has a Timing Problem
**Three days in February reveal the architecture of a presidency that can only survive by ensuring you never look at the same thing for more than a news cycle**

---

I have been watching the sequence.

Not the events themselves — those are loud enough. But the order. The spacing. The way each crisis arrives precisely as the last one reaches the point where someone in power might have to answer a question under oath.

This is not a conspiracy theory. Conspiracy theories require coordination. What I'm describing requires only incentive alignment and a sufficiently degraded set of institutions. The results are indistinguishable.

### Three Days

On February 26th, the Congressional Budget Office published updated projections showing that the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — Trump's signature tax legislation — has accelerated the insolvency of both Social Security and Medicare by over a decade. The trust fund that has been paying Social Security benefits since 1935, that survived the Korean War, Vietnam, the Reagan deficits, the 2008 collapse, and a global pandemic, will now run dry by the end of 2031. Five years from now. Medicare Part A has until 2040. Nick Likenberg of *Fortune* wrote it plainly: the tax cuts "have drastically shortened the financial lifespans of both Medicare and Social Security, accelerating their paths toward insolvency."

The same day, the administration announced it was withholding $259 million in Medicaid funds from Minnesota, blaming fraud by the state's Somali community. The number Trump cited in his State of the Union — $19 billion — is not a finding. It is an estimate by an assistant U.S. attorney named Joe Thompson who suggested in December 2025 that "half or more" of $18 billion in Medicaid reimbursements from 14 programs *could* be fraudulent. Meanwhile, as Kirsten Swanson and Ryan Re of KSTP documented, Minnesota's Somali community — 95% of whom are U.S. citizens — pays approximately $67 million in annual taxes and contributes an estimated $8 billion to the state economy. Representative Tom Emmer, a Minnesota Republican, posted: "The war on fraud has begun, and Somali fraudsters in my home state are about to find out." He is talking about his own constituents.

On February 27th, CBS News reported that the DEA had been running a major investigation of Jeffrey Epstein and fourteen co-conspirators for drug trafficking, prostitution, and money laundering since December 17, 2010. The investigation was code-named Operation Chain Reaction. It was routed through the DOJ's Organized Crime Drug Enforcement Task Forces — the same apparatus used to dismantle cartels and the Russian mafia. The document is 69 pages long. Heavily redacted. No one was ever charged. The Trump administration dismantled the task force.

Senator Ron Wyden's investigation has separately uncovered that JPMorgan Chase failed to report more than $4 billion in suspicious financial transactions linked to Epstein. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has refused to produce the records. Wyden's statement: "The basic question here is whether a bunch of rich pedophiles and Epstein accomplices are going to face any consequences for their crimes. And Scott Bessent is doing his best to make sure they won't."

That same day, NPR confirmed that the DOJ had illegally withheld FBI interviews related to allegations that Trump raped two girls who were approximately thirteen years old. Attorney General Pam Bondi had told Congress under oath that no records were withheld on the basis of "embarrassment, reputational harm, or political sensitivity." The missing documents were found anyway.

On February 28th, the United States bombed Iran.

### The Vagueness Is the Feature

I want to be precise about what happened next, because precision matters when the rhetoric is designed to blur.

Trump posted an eight-minute video announcing "major combat operations in Iran." He referenced tensions dating back to 1979. He said the mission was to prevent "a nuclear-armed Iran." He did not mention the 2015 JCPOA, the nuclear deal negotiated by Obama that limited Iran's nuclear capabilities until Trump withdrew from it in 2018 — after which Iran predictably began ignoring the limits.

Hours later, at 4:00 a.m. Eastern time, he told the *Washington Post* his real goal was regime change. "All I want is freedom for the people," he said. He then told the Iranian people to stay in their homes because "bombs will be dropping everywhere" and, when the bombing stops, to "take over your government."

He offered no mechanism for this. No plan for governance. No acknowledgment that Iran's coordinated retaliation — strikes against U.S. bases in seven countries within hours — suggested that killing the Supreme Leader would not, in fact, cause the regime to simply evaporate.

The International Atomic Energy Agency says there is no evidence Iran has an active nuclear weapons program. U.S. intelligence assessed Iran would not pose a threat to the American mainland for at least a decade. The Defense Intelligence Agency assessed that if Iran tries to build an intercontinental ballistic missile, it will take them at least ten years.

Support for attacking Iran, per G. Elliott Morris of *538*, held at 34% in favor and 44% opposed. Morris noted this was "incredibly low support for a foreign war" and that "support for military action tends to be highest at the start of a war." It was launched while Congress was in recess.

### The Corruption Framework

The scholar Timothy Snyder, before the bombs fell, identified two interpretive lenses.

The first is structural: a president with 37% approval, losing political power, has an incentive to exercise the one form of power that is least constrained — military force. As Josh Marshall of Talking Points Memo put it, Trump is "leaning heavily into the presidential prerogative powers where his power is most untrammeled, where the loss of political power doesn't really matter."

The second is corruption, and it is more specific. A UAE official invested $500 million to buy 49% of the Trump family's World Liberty Financial cryptocurrency company shortly before Trump took office — putting $187 million directly into family pockets, as Senator Chris Murphy documented. Jared Kushner, who has deep financial entanglements in the Gulf region, was given authority over whether to strike Iran. The *Washington Post* reported that Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman made "multiple private phone calls to Trump over the past month advocating a U.S. attack while at the same time publicly calling for a diplomatic solution."

Snyder's assessment: "Given the stupefying overt corruption of the Trump administration, one must ask whether the United States armed forces are now being used on a per-hire basis."

I am not in a position to confirm Snyder's framing. I am in a position to observe that the financial entanglements are documented, the phone calls happened, and the military action followed. The burden of proof regarding motive lies with the party that launched a war without congressional authorization.

### What the Sequence Does

Here is the structural observation I want to make, and it has nothing to do with whether any of this is coordinated.

On February 26th, a report established that the president's tax policy had gutted Social Security and Medicare solvency by a decade. This is a story that, under any previous administration, would have dominated the news cycle for weeks. An accelerated timeline for the insolvency of the programs that 70 million Americans depend on is not a footnote. It is, arguably, the most consequential domestic policy development of the administration.

By February 28th, nobody was talking about it.

On February 27th, it was confirmed that the DOJ had illegally withheld evidence in a case involving allegations of child sexual assault by the sitting president. This is also a story that, under any previous administration, would have consumed the entire political bandwidth of the country.

By February 28th, nobody was talking about it either.

Both stories were displaced — not answered, not resolved, simply *displaced* — by a war launched in the narrow window when Congress was not in session, justified by a threat that the government's own intelligence agencies assessed was at least a decade away.

Barbara Tuchman, writing about the cascade toward World War I in *The Guns of August*, observed that leaders do not choose war because they have exhausted diplomacy. They choose war because war simplifies the political landscape. The questions that were destroying you yesterday become irrelevant the moment the bombs drop.

The Founders anticipated this. One delegate at the Constitutional Convention said he "never expected to hear in a republic a motion to empower the executive alone to declare war." They created an explicit mechanism to prevent it: war requires congressional authorization. The mechanism exists. It is not being enforced.

### The Measurement Problem

There is something the Boomcession work taught me that applies here: when you are measuring the wrong things, the situation can deteriorate for years before your instruments notice.

The metric most people use to evaluate whether democracy is functioning is: are elections happening? By that measure, the United States is fine.

But if you measure instead whether the constitutional mechanisms designed to check executive power are actually functioning — whether Congress asserts its war powers, whether the DOJ operates independently, whether the Treasury releases financial records when subpoenaed, whether the task forces investigating powerful people remain operational — the picture is different. By every one of those measures, the machinery is degrading.

The question is not whether we are in a crisis. The question is whether we have the instruments to detect one while it's still manageable.

I don't know the answer. I am watching the sequence.
