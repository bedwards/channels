# The Decapitation Delusion
**Why Operation Epic Fury reveals every failure of American strategic thinking since 2001 — compressed into a single weekend**

---

### The Clean Cut That Never Stays Clean

Three American soldiers are dead. The Ayatollah is almost certainly dead. The IRGC's senior command structure has been vaporized. And Donald Trump is already looking for the exit.

Within hours of the most devastating first strike against a sovereign nation since Shock and Awe, the United States reportedly reached out through mediators to propose an immediate ceasefire. Iran rejected it outright. This is worth sitting with for a moment: the president launched a regime change war at 2 a.m. on a Saturday, informed nobody in Congress, declared that "American heroes may be lost" — his words — and by breakfast was apparently hoping the whole thing would wrap itself up like Venezuela.

It won't. And the reason it won't has nothing to do with Iranian strength or American weakness. It has to do with a delusion that has survived every lesson the last twenty-five years tried to teach us: that you can sever the head and the body will simply comply.

### The Doctrine That Won't Die

The decapitation fantasy goes back at least to Manuel Noriega. It accelerated through Saddam Hussein, Muammar Gaddafi, and the endless parade of "high-value targets" in Afghanistan. The pattern is identical every time: remove the top, and the structure beneath will either collapse in your favor or reconstitute as something more docile. It has never worked as designed. Not once.

In Iraq, the Ba'athist command structure didn't disappear when Saddam fell — it metastasized into the insurgency that killed 4,500 Americans. In Libya, Gaddafi's death didn't produce the democratic transition that Samantha Power and Susan Rice had sketched on their whiteboards — it produced a failed state with open-air slave markets. In Afghanistan, we spent twenty years building an alternative government that evaporated in eleven days.

What the decapitation doctrine actually produces is not regime change but regime collapse — and the distinction matters enormously. As Dr. Trita Parsi of the Quincy Institute put it bluntly on Saturday morning: "In regime change, you're actively trying to install a new government and their track record becomes your track record. In regime collapse, all you're doing is getting rid of the existing one and then you say you have nothing to do with whatever comes afterwards."

This is not a minor semantic difference. It is the entire game. The Trump administration does not want to own post-war Iran any more than it wanted to own post-war Venezuela. The plan, such as it is, appears to be: destroy the regime, broadcast Voice of America in Farsi telling people to rise up, install Reza Pahlavi's son as a symbolic figurehead from his comfortable perch in Maryland, and then wash your hands when it all goes sideways.

### The Sugar High Miscalculation

How did we get here when seemingly every institution with any stake in the outcome opposed it? The military brass leaked their objections to the press for months. The Gulf allies who now host American bases didn't want it. European allies said nothing publicly but privately flagged their horror. Even within MAGA world, the "no more forever wars" contingent represented what was supposed to be the animating foreign policy principle of the entire movement.

The answer appears to involve a compound of several toxic inputs.

First, Venezuela. Operation Midnight Hammer went off without a single American casualty. Trump rolled the dice, won, and concluded he was operating in a dimension where conventional strategic wisdom simply didn't apply. Every adviser who told him "you can't do that" over the past decade — move the embassy, give Israel the Golan, assassinate Soleimani, bomb Fordo — turned out, in his calculation, to be wrong. Why would Iran be different?

Second, Israeli intelligence briefings. In a December 29th meeting, according to multiple reports, Netanyahu's team presented Iran as far weaker than it actually is and convinced Trump that this was a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity. Kenneth Pollack documented this phenomenon in *The Threatening Storm* — the way intelligence can be shaped to confirm what the listener already wants to hear. Trump heard that the Iranians would fold. He did not hear — or did not register — that the IRGC has planned for exactly this scenario and pre-authorized retaliatory strike packages across the entire Gulf.

Third, the uncomfortable question nobody in mainstream media wants to touch: the Epstein files. Trump has blocked the full release mandated by his own signed legislation. Ghislaine Maxwell has been moved to a minimum-security facility. Whatever those files contain about Trump's relationship with Jeffrey Epstein — and whatever Israeli intelligence knows about them — they represent a variable that no standard geopolitical model accounts for. This isn't conspiracy theorizing; it's acknowledging that a president who ran on "no more forever wars" just launched the most ambitious regime change operation in American history, against the advice of his own military, for the benefit of a foreign government. The political math doesn't add up without a hidden term.

### A Regional War in Real Time

The Iranian response Saturday was not the calibrated, face-saving gesture of April 2024. It was immediate, widespread, and escalatory.

Ballistic missiles targeted the USS Abraham Lincoln. Suicide drones struck the US naval base in Bahrain — the Fifth Fleet's home port — and footage shows American service members reacting in real-time shock as the explosion hits. Airports in Dubai, Abu Dhabi, and Kuwait took drone strikes, stranding hundreds of thousands of international passengers. A missile evaded Iron Dome and struck a shelter in Beit Shemesh, killing eight people, including children. Downtown Tel Aviv has been hit.

The Iranians struck a girls' school in Hormozgan Province. The United States or Israel — reporting suggests the latter — struck a girls' school in Manab, killing at least forty-eight students. On day one.

Oil futures spiked 10% by Saturday morning, with analysts projecting $100/barrel from a $72 close on Friday. War risk insurance for ships in the Strait of Hormuz jumped 50% instantly. DP World suspended Dubai port operations — the ninth busiest port on Earth. MSC and Maersk halted all Middle East bookings. The global supply chain choke point that every defense strategist has war-gamed for decades is now active.

Here is the part that the "preemptive strike" framing does not survive: the Iranians had already offered a deal that exceeded the JCPOA. The Omani foreign minister confirmed it publicly. Full surrender of enriched uranium stockpiles. IAEA inspectors. An end to the entire enrichment dispute. Trump's response was to bomb them the next day while Jared Kushner's negotiation team — two real estate developers with no nuclear expertise — pretended the talks were serious.

Senator Tim Kaine put it in terms that broke through the usual congressional mush: "Is he too mentally incapacitated to realize we had a diplomatic agreement with Iran that was keeping its nuclear program in check until he ripped it up during the first term? These strikes are a colossal mistake." Ro Khanna and Thomas Massie demanded an immediate war powers vote. Mark Warner, representing the Democratic leadership's instinct for procedural hand-wringing, asked for "scrutiny."

### The Biblical Angle Nobody Is Laughing About

CNN Jerusalem reported — and this is not satire, this is their actual reporting — that the timing of the attack carries "symbolic meaning in Judaism." The strike coincided with the reading of the Zakhor passage from Deuteronomy, which commands the destruction of Amalek, Israel's "archetypal enemy." The date was set weeks ago.

So: American war planners did not choose the date. Israeli war planners chose the date to align with a biblical commandment to annihilate an ancient enemy. And the United States followed. JD Vance, the man who built his political brand on opposing precisely this kind of war, offered this: "I think we have to avoid overlearning the lessons of the past."

That sentence will age the way "Mission Accomplished" aged. Because the lesson of Iraq wasn't a lesson you could overlearn. You either learned it or you launched another war. Vance chose the latter.

### What No Offramp Actually Means

The structural problem is not that this war will go badly — it's that there is no definition of success that doesn't require something nobody can deliver.

If the goal is regime collapse, you get Libya. Civil war, refugee crisis, weapons dispersal across the region, a power vacuum that every faction from the IRGC remnants to ISIS to Kurdish separatists will rush to fill. The Trump administration has made clear, through internal conversations, that "no one wants to take responsibility for what happens to Iran afterwards." This is the lesson of Iraq that Vance thinks we overlearned.

If the goal is negotiated settlement, you have a credibility problem that may be terminal. You used diplomacy as a ruse. You bombed a country the day after it offered to surrender its nuclear program. As Parsi noted, "nothing would serve the Israeli interest more than to completely destroy America's credibility as a negotiating partner." That credibility is now gone. Future adversaries and allies alike have watched what happened to a country that tried to negotiate in good faith with the United States under Trump. The message is: don't bother.

If the goal is the Pahlavi restoration that Voice of America is currently broadcasting into Tehran, you are betting the stability of a nation of ninety million people on a diaspora prince who has never set foot on Iranian soil as its leader, installed by the country that just killed schoolchildren. Thomas Jefferson himself could not make that work.

### The Interceptor Problem Nobody Is Discussing

There is a detail buried in Saturday's chaos that should terrify anyone paying attention. In the twelve-day war of last summer — which was billed as a limited engagement — the United States burned through 25% of its missile interceptor stockpile. These interceptors take years to manufacture and replace.

We are now defending not just Israel but bases in Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan, and across the Gulf against a sustained Iranian missile and drone campaign. The Iranians are estimated to have thousands of missiles remaining, with reconstitution assistance from China. If we are at 75% depletion in a matter of weeks, our ability to defend our own forces — let alone allies — degrades to the point where simple math becomes the deciding factor in this war.

This is what happens when you confuse a capability advantage with an unlimited one. The arsenal is finite. The enemy knows exactly how to make you spend it.

### What Lingers

Here is the thing that will keep me up tonight, and I think should keep you up too.

Three American families got the phone call today. Three families who were told — who were promised, by a man who ran on peace — that their sons or daughters were safe. Operation Epic Fury. A name that sounds like a rejected Call of Duty expansion pack, attached to real people who are really dead now, for a war that began because one country's prime minister chose the date from the Book of Deuteronomy, and another country's president thought regime change would be as easy as it was in Venezuela.

Eighty percent of Americans opposed this war before it started. Congress never voted. The diplomatic deal was already on the table. And the people who sold themselves as the anti-war movement — Tulsi Gabbard, JD Vance, Steven Miller — are now explaining why this time is different, why the lessons of the past were overlearned, why freedom is worth the sacrifice they'll never personally make.

It's not different. It's never been different. The only thing that changes is the name of the country we're about to spend twenty years pretending we can fix.
