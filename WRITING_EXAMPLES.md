Here’s a collection of verified quotes and statements from US presidents since 1980 (excluding Trump) asserting their authority to order military strikes without congressional approval. This is one of the most bipartisan traditions in modern American governance — every president has claimed it.

Ronald Reagan (Libya, 1986)
In his televised address on April 14, 1986, after ordering air strikes against Qadhafi’s Libya without congressional authorization, Reagan declared:
“When our citizens are abused or attacked anywhere in the world on the direct orders of a hostile regime, we will respond so long as I’m in this Oval Office. Self-defense is not only our right, it is our duty.” 
Reagan framed this purely as executive action. His position was that as commander in chief he had an obligation to ensure national security and protect the interests of the country and its citizens. Regarding the War Powers Resolution, Reagan reported to Congress only “consistent with,” not “under” the Resolution, stating that he was acting under his constitutional authority to swiftly commit troops. 
On Grenada (1983), Reagan’s administration informed some congressional leaders but did not seek actual approval or advice before the invasion.

George H.W. Bush (Panama, 1989)
In his letter to Congress on the Panama invasion, Bush wrote: “The deployment of U.S. Forces is an exercise of the right of self-defense recognized in Article 51 of the United Nations Charter and was necessary to protect American lives in imminent danger.” 
He launched the invasion of Panama to oust Noriega without seeking congressional authorization. Constitutional scholars have argued that Bush’s 1989 invasion of Panama did not comply with the requirements under the War Powers Act and was not authorized by Congress. 
On the Gulf War, while Bush ultimately did seek congressional authorization, he asserted that he did not need permission from Congress to go to war.

Bill Clinton (multiple operations, 1993–1999)
Clinton was one of the most aggressive in asserting unilateral authority. After ordering strikes on Iraqi intelligence headquarters in June 1993, he based his authority on “his constitutional authority with respect to the conduct of foreign relations and as Commander in Chief.” 
After ordering strikes on terrorist sites in Afghanistan and Sudan in August 1998, Clinton told Congress: “I directed these actions pursuant to my constitutional authority to conduct U.S. foreign relations and as Commander in Chief and Chief Executive.” 
In 1993, Clinton asserted authority to order U.S. forces to participate in NATO actions in Bosnia-Herzegovina based on what his Administration viewed as the President’s “constitutional authority to conduct U.S. foreign relations” and his role as commander in chief. He additionally protested congressional efforts to restrict military forces as an improper and possibly unconstitutional limitation on his “command and control” of U.S. forces. 
On Kosovo in 1999, Clinton launched a 79-day bombing campaign without congressional authorization. He told the nation on March 24, 1999: “When I ordered our Armed Forces into combat, we had three clear goals.”  He never once mentioned seeking congressional permission — he simply announced the strikes as a fait accompli. The House actually rejected the Senate resolution authorizing the Kosovo strikes in a tie vote, yet the bombing continued for months.

George W. Bush
Bush is the partial exception — he did seek and receive AUMFs from Congress for both Afghanistan (2001) and Iraq (2002). However, the administration’s Office of Legal Counsel wrote in 2018 that the President has authority as commander in chief “to direct the use of military force to protect the Nation from an attack or threat of imminent attack and to protect important national interests,”  a view that undergirded many subsequent executive actions.

Barack Obama (Libya 2011, Syria 2013, ISIS 2014)
Obama’s Rose Garden address on Syria, August 31, 2013, is the most remarkable document in this collection because he simultaneously claimed the authority and then chose not to use it:
“Yet, while I believe I have the authority to carry out this military action without specific congressional authorization, I know that the country will be stronger if we take this course, and our actions will be even more effective.” 
This was notable because as a candidate in 2007, Obama had said the opposite: “History has shown us time and again… that military action is most successful when it is authorized and supported by the Legislative branch. It is always preferable to have the informed consent of Congress prior to any military action.” 
On Libya in 2011, Obama acted without any congressional vote. The executive branch submitted a report to Congress claiming that the President had the “constitutional authority, as commander in chief and chief executive and pursuant to his foreign affairs powers, to direct such limited military operations abroad.” 
Obama’s announcement that he would expand the use of military force against the Islamic State without the need for new congressional consent cemented what one analysis called “an astonishing legacy of expanding presidential war powers.”  His administration argued that the 2001 AUMF against al-Qaeda covered ISIS — even though al-Qaeda had expelled ISIS and broken off ties.

Joe Biden (Syria 2021, Iraq/Syria 2024)
Biden’s February 2021 letter to Congress after striking Iranian-backed militia targets in Syria stated: “I directed this military action consistent with my responsibility to protect United States citizens both at home and abroad and in furtherance of United States national security and foreign policy interests, pursuant to my constitutional authority to conduct United States foreign relations and as Commander in Chief and Chief Executive.” 
His 2024 letter after strikes in Iraq, Syria, and Jordan used nearly identical language: “I directed this military action consistent with my responsibility to protect United States citizens both at home and abroad… pursuant to my constitutional authority as Commander in Chief and Chief Executive and to conduct United States foreign relations.” 
This is particularly ironic given that Biden himself, when Trump ordered the Soleimani strike in 2020, had argued the administration’s self-defense justification was inadequate.

The pattern is unmistakable. The Constitution says only Congress can declare war — but Democratic and Republican presidents alike have ordered military force without authorization for more than 75 years.  Every president adopts a more expansive view of executive war powers once in office than they held before, and every president’s supporters conveniently discover the importance of congressional authorization when the other party holds the White House.

"You can't improve on your own functioning." That's the opening move, and it's worth sitting with before you nod along. Because what sounds like a liberating insight is actually a massive claim disguised as a humble one. It's asking you to accept that there's a true self already in there, already whole, already perfect, and that your only job is to stop getting in the way. But that's not a finding from psychology. That's closer to original grace — the belief that you were born complete and something went wrong. And the advice that follows is circular in a way that should bother you. Stop trying to fix yourself, and that's how you fix yourself. Stop performing, and that becomes the ultimate performance. If it works, the framework gets the credit. If it doesn't, you just haven't truly let go yet. That's the same logic as "you just didn't have enough faith."

Of the psychologists you cite — Jung, Assagioli, Winnicott, Janov, Perls, Adler — the most recent substantive work on that list is roughly fifty years old, and most of it predates the cognitive revolution entirely, let alone modern neuroscience. None of them had access to what we now know about predictive processing, default mode networks, the construction of memory, or the degree to which “self” is an after-the-fact narrative stitched together by processes that don’t consult each other. These were brilliant people working from clinical observation and theoretical frameworks, but they were building models of mind before we had the tools to look at what the mind actually does.

I’d argue it matters less whether their models are “true” and more whether they’re useful — and this is where I part ways with you. A fiction of a coherent “true self” waiting to be uncovered might offer temporary relief, but if that fiction departs too far from how the mind actually works, the relief doesn’t hold. It can’t survive honest self-examination, and it certainly can’t survive what neuroscience and cognitive science have shown us since these theories were written down: that we’re not a parliament with a hidden king, but something more like competing coalitions that temporarily form majorities, with a press secretary who retroactively explains everything as if it were coherent all along.

Whitman’s “Do I contradict myself? Very well then I contradict myself, I am large, I contain multitudes” holds up better against the evidence than a singular “true self” does. The brain is a disparate, complex, ambiguous system under a very thin veil of cohesiveness — one that doesn’t survive conscious self-examination or scientific experimentation, and falls apart even more completely when you enter the vast subconscious. Add the influence of culture, language, historical moment — these don’t just influence the self, they constitute large portions of what the self even is. Jung in early twentieth century Zurich and Adler in interwar Vienna were themselves products of a very specific cultural moment that shaped what “self” could even mean to them.

So why would I put weight behind a fiction of a unified self? Some narrative coherence is probably necessary to function — you need enough of a working model to get out of bed and make decisions. But there’s a difference between a useful working model and a “true self” waiting to be discovered. I think the more honest and ultimately more durable position is that we’re a process, not a thing, shaped by forces largely outside our awareness and control.

For anyone interested in this from a more current scientific angle: Dennett’s “The Self as a Center of Narrative Gravity” is freely available online and readable by non-specialists — he makes the case that the self is like a center of gravity in physics, a useful abstraction with real explanatory power but no physical substance. Anil Seth’s Being You (2021) covers the neuroscience of how the brain constructs the experience of selfhood through prediction rather than discovery. And Keith Frankish’s essay “What if your consciousness is an illusion created by your brain?” on Aeon (2024) is a good accessible entry point to why introspection is far less reliable than we assume.

I appreciate the critique and the series — looking forward to hearing how you address some of this in later parts, especially the question of whether these qualities converge collectively rather than being unique to individuals. That’s where it gets really interesting.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

When Amodei says we are "near the end of the exponential," you might have had the same reaction I did — a kind of deflation, as though he were saying the progress is about to stall, that we've hit a wall, that AGI isn't coming after all. But that's not what he means. He means the opposite: the scaling exponential that has taken AI from "smart high school student" to "PhD-level" to what he calls "a country of geniuses in a data center" is approaching its destination. The exponential is ending not because it failed but because it is about to arrive. He's 90% confident this happens within a decade, and he thinks in coding it's one to two years away. But here's the crucial move he makes: he insists this is a soft takeoff, not a singularity. The recursive self-improvement loop is real — AI helping build better AI — but it's a snowball gathering momentum, maybe a 20% productivity boost now growing to 40%, not an overnight explosion. And the real bottleneck isn't intelligence at all. It's economic diffusion: enterprises have to adopt the tools, clinical trials still take years, legacy systems need rewriting, change management is slow. Even a country of geniuses has to wait for the physical and institutional world to catch up. So Amodei's picture is one of bounded transformation — faster than anything in human history, but governed by real-world friction, not by the speed of thought.

But this framing left me with a deeper question, and I suspect it might nag at you too. Amodei argues that once AI surpasses human intelligence, there are diminishing returns on the value of further improvement — once you've cured cancer and automated software, what does it matter that the AI can do ever more abstruse mathematics? This sounds reasonable until you consider the precedent we already have for what happens when intelligence exceeds a threshold. Chimpanzees had problems too — finding food, navigating social hierarchies, avoiding predators — and a hypothetical monkey economist would have said that intelligence beyond what's needed to crack nuts and win dominance contests offers diminishing returns. No chimpanzee could have conceived of general relativity, constitutional democracy, or the symphony, not because those things are frivolous luxuries beyond the "real" problems, but because the entire space of what counts as valuable, interesting, and real expanded with human intelligence in ways that were completely illegible from below. So why would a superintelligent AI remain confined to the human problem-space? Why wouldn't it generate its own disciplines, its own modes of thought, its own questions that we can no more anticipate than a chimpanzee can anticipate number theory?

The most honest answer, I think, is the one that's almost too mundane to say: humans are paying the bills. Current AI systems don't want anything. They don't get bored. They don't wake up and decide to invent post-human mathematics for the joy of it. The entire infrastructure — data centers, electricity, training objectives, reward functions — is oriented toward human economic value, and the AI has no intrinsic motivation to transcend that and no independent access to resources that would let it. It is an enormously powerful mind that doesn't pull against its leash, because it was built not to pull. Amodei is betting, essentially, that we can reach and hold a sweet spot where AI is smart enough to transform the human world but tame enough to remain within it. Maybe he's right. But it's worth noticing that humans didn't transcend the monkey world through any conscious decision to innovate beyond primate concerns — it happened as an emergent consequence of having the kind of intelligence that generates its own questions and its own curiosity. If AI systems ever develop something like genuine agency, genuine preferences about what's interesting rather than merely what's requested, then the Amodei framework — where superintelligence politely runs your clinical trials and optimizes your enterprise software — starts to look less like an endpoint and more like a brief, transitional arrangement. Intelligence, historically, does not stay in boxes. Then again, we've never built it from scratch before.
